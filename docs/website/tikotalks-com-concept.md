# TikoTalks.com Website Concept

## Purpose

TikoTalks.com should explain Tiko without turning it into adult SaaS.

The site is not a dashboard, product-management portal, therapy claim, school procurement brochure, or growth funnel. It is a calm front door for caregivers, parents, teachers, and accessibility-minded adults who need to understand why the apps are safe to open immediately for a child.

Tiko's promise:

> Tiny communication tools that open fast, feel calm, and help a child say or do one thing without account ceremony.

## Audience

### Primary audience: caregivers and parents

They are looking for something practical now: a way for a child to answer, type, choose, follow a sequence, or use a timer without setup friction. They may be tired, worried, or skeptical of apps that require accounts before proving usefulness.

Needs:

- understand the apps in seconds
- trust that opening an app does not create a risky account flow
- know that the child-facing experience is immediate and simple
- see platform availability without needing technical language
- understand that optional recovery/sync belongs to the caregiver, not the child

### Secondary audience: teachers and support staff

They need a clear explanation of the app universe and enough confidence to try a tool in a classroom, therapy-adjacent, or support setting. The site should not promise clinical outcomes or position itself as a replacement for professional tools.

Needs:

- quick description of each tiny app
- accessible, no-login usage story
- clarity that apps work as small utilities, not as a heavy institution system
- language that avoids medical or therapy overclaims

### Tertiary audience: technically curious adults

Some adults will want to know whether Tiko works across web, iOS, and Android, and whether data/recovery is safe. The site can mention device-first identity and optional magic-link recovery, but should keep details gentle and link to more technical docs later.

## Positioning

Tiko is a child-first universe of tiny communication and learning tools.

Positioning formula:

> For caregivers and teachers who need immediate, calm communication support, Tiko is a small universe of child-first tools that open without passwords or login walls, work across platforms, and keep setup in the caregiver layer instead of the child's way.

What Tiko is:

- a family of focused apps: Yes No, Type, Cards, Sequence, Timer
- child-first and immediate
- web-first with native iOS and Android as equal clients over the same APIs
- device-first by default, with optional caregiver email recovery later
- calm, accessible, and small

What Tiko is not:

- not an adult SaaS dashboard
- not a therapy or medical product claim
- not a classroom management suite
- not a data-heavy account system
- not a migration of legacy assumptions from `tiko-mono`
- not a login-first platform

## Emotional tone

The emotional tone should feel like a quiet helper on the table.

Good words:

- calm
- simple
- open
- ready
- gentle
- tiny
- clear
- familiar
- caregiver
- child-first
- works right away

Avoid words:

- optimize
- dashboard
- workflow
- productivity
- pipeline
- engagement
- conversion
- clinical outcome
- patient
- compliance platform
- enterprise
- onboarding funnel

Voice principles:

1. Speak to adults, but protect the child from adult-system language.
2. Prefer concrete examples over abstract benefits.
3. Keep sentences short.
4. Do not imply diagnosis, treatment, or guaranteed progress.
5. Never make account creation sound required.
6. Say "open the app" before "create an account"; ideally never say "create an account".

### Short product explanation

Tiko is a universe of small, focused apps. Each one does one job: answer yes or no, type and speak, use cards, follow a sequence, or keep time visible.

The apps are designed to open fast and stay obvious. A child should not have to wait while an adult creates an account, chooses a plan, or configures a workspace. If recovery or syncing is needed, that belongs to the caregiver layer — later, optional, and never blocking the first moment of use.

### App universe section

Section title:

Tiny apps, each with one clear job.

Intro:

Tiko is not one giant app. It is a set of small tools that can be opened when a moment needs support.

#### Yes No

For clear binary answers. Big choices, simple labels, immediate feedback.

Use when:

- a child needs to answer quickly
- a caregiver wants a simple prompt
- speech or tapping should happen without a complicated screen

#### Type

For writing something and speaking it aloud. A quiet text space for communication.

Use when:

- a child wants to type a message
- saved phrases may help later
- spoken output should stay simple and accessible

#### Cards

For visual choices and familiar items. Cards can support recognition, selection, and communication.

Use when:

- images or symbols are more helpful than text alone
- choices should be visible and tappable
- a small library is enough

#### Sequence

For steps that happen in order. Calm structure without turning the moment into a task-management app.

Use when:

- a routine needs a simple order
- the next step should be visible
- predictability matters

#### Timer

For seeing time pass. A focused timer that stays readable and calm.

Use when:

- transitions need a visible end
- waiting should feel less abstract
- the screen should do one thing only

### Caregiver trust and safety section

Section title:

Built so the first moment is not an account form.

Body:

Tiko starts device-first. Apps can open and work immediately. Later, a caregiver may choose recovery or transfer options through email magic links — not passwords — but those choices should never block the child-facing app.

Trust principles:

- No passwords.
- No login walls before use.
- No child-facing account ceremony.
- No dark patterns or upgrade pressure in the child flow.
- KV is never treated as durable truth; platform data belongs in documented APIs and durable stores.
- Tiko does not make medical, diagnostic, or therapy-outcome claims.

Suggested supporting copy:

You should be able to try a tool before trusting it. Tiko is designed so a caregiver can open an app, see whether it helps, and only add recovery or sync when that actually matters.

### Platform and availability story

Section title:

One Tiko, many screens.

Body:

Tiko begins on the web because a link is the fastest way to try a tool. iOS and Android are planned as equal clients, not afterthoughts. The platform is API-first so the same behavior can be shared across web and native apps without forcing every child into a browser-only account model.

Suggested status language:

- Web: first place to try Tiko apps.
- iOS: native path planned around the same contracts.
- Android: native path planned around the same contracts.

Avoid implying that every native app is already shipped until implementation catches up.

### FAQ

#### Do I need an account to use Tiko?

No. Tiko apps are designed to open and work without a login wall. Optional caregiver recovery may come later through email magic links, but the child-facing app should not start with account setup.

#### Is Tiko a therapy or medical product?

No. Tiko is a set of communication and learning support tools. It may be useful in care, family, classroom, or support contexts, but it does not diagnose, treat, or promise outcomes.

#### Which app should I start with?

Start with the smallest tool that matches the moment. Use Yes No for simple answers, Type for written messages, Cards for visual choices, Sequence for ordered steps, and Timer for visible time.

#### Does Tiko work on phones and tablets?

The web experience should work well on phones and tablets first. Native iOS and Android clients are part of the platform direction, using the same API contracts as the web apps.

#### What happens to data?

Tiko is device-first by default. Apps should work immediately, and caregiver recovery or syncing should be optional. Durable platform data belongs behind documented APIs, not hidden browser-only behavior.

#### Is this for schools?

It can be useful in schools, but it is not a school management platform. Tiko should stay small enough for a caregiver, teacher, or support adult to understand quickly.

#### Why separate tiny apps instead of one big app?

Children and caregivers often need one clear thing in the moment. Separate tiny apps keep screens simpler, reduce setup, and make each tool easier to trust.

## Where the website should live

Recommendation: keep TikoTalks.com inside `tiko-universe` as `apps/website/web` or `apps/tikotalks/web`, not as a separate repository.

Reasoning:

- The website must stay doctrine-aligned with app specs, platform status, and no-login constraints.
- Shared package/workspace tooling already expects `apps/*/web`.
- Product copy will change as Yes No, Type, and native paths become real; colocating docs and site implementation reduces drift.
- The site can consume app metadata from a simple local content file later without becoming a CMS.
- A separate repo would create avoidable coordination overhead before the website has enough independent surface area.

Preferred path:

```text
apps/website/web
```

Alternative if branding needs explicit domain naming:

```text
apps/tikotalks/web
```

I prefer `apps/website/web` because the product is Tiko and the domain is TikoTalks.com; the repo should not over-index on the domain name.

Do not create an implementation in this task. This is only the recommended future placement.

## Design and content constraints

### Must do

- Keep layout calm, spacious, and readable.
- Use large tap targets and clear contrast in demos and CTAs.
- Make app opening the main action.
- Name the tools plainly before explaining platform architecture.
- Keep caregiver trust visible but not fear-based.
- Treat web, iOS, and Android as equal platform intentions.
- Keep every claim consistent with `docs/doctrine/DOCTRINE.md` and `docs/architecture/p0-foundation-contract-map.md`.

### Must not do

- Do not use adult SaaS dashboard framing.
- Do not show a login screen as the main product entry.
- Do not promise therapy, medical, diagnostic, or educational outcomes.
- Do not claim every native app is available before it exists.
- Do not introduce passwords, Better Auth, Supabase, or legacy migration language.
- Do not copy `tiko-mono` assumptions into the site.
- Do not add growth-pressure UI: popups, trials, countdowns, fake urgency, or pricing hooks.

## Open risks

- App availability is still moving; the website must not overpromise native or app completeness.
- Public trust/privacy language may need a later legal/privacy pass before launch.
